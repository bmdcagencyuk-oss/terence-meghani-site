Two monitors, a Lenovo and an iMac, crammed into a bay window in my bedroom in Ilford. Cables in every direction. Tearsheets and reference prints pinned to the column between the windows — which was better than nothing and worse than a proper board.

![The original 2014 home studio: two monitors in a bay window in a bedroom in Ilford, mood-board pinned to the column between the windows.](/notes/bay-window-2014.webp)

*The actual room. Ilford, late 2014.*

It was 2014. I'd just been let go after five years at the same company. I had a camera bag I used at weekends to shoot weddings, and I had an idea scratched into a notebook. I opened Photoshop, drew a gorilla, and by the end of that week Krazy Media was registered.

That's it. That's the founding story. No investors. No co-founder retreat. No strategic pivot. Just a bay window in a bedroom in Ilford and a deadline I'd invented for myself.

Twelve years later, I'm still going.

## What I got wrong

For the first few years, I said yes to everything.

Someone needed a logo. Yes. A print campaign. Yes. Event photography — fine. Social media management, brochure design, a radio ad once, for a client who asked with such quiet confidence that it didn't occur to me to question whether I had the first clue about radio production. I said yes. I worked it out.

In the early days, that's pragmatism, not arrogance. You need work. You take what's available and you build the skills around the brief. I don't regret any of it.

What I got wrong was letting it calcify into a business model.

I was good enough at most things that no one complained. Clients came back. Referrals came in. But I was working at a comfortable distance from what I actually cared about — which was the craft of building things on screen. *Brand systems.* Websites. The *why* underneath the visual. That kept me up at night in the good way. The radio ad kept me up at night in the other way.

The cost wasn't visible for a while. It showed up a few years in, when I'd look at a portfolio full of technically competent work and feel oddly disconnected from it. I'd built things for clients I liked, at rates that seemed fair, and still felt like I was standing slightly to the left of where I should be.

What changed it wasn't a single decision. It was slower than that. I started choosing the interesting brief over the convenient one, consistently enough that the interesting briefs eventually became the majority. I didn't pivot. I just became more deliberate about which yeses I gave.

## What I got right

I believed I was good. That sounds obvious, but it's actually quite rare.

A lot of people I know from those early years have moved on. Not because they weren't talented — some of them were genuinely gifted — but because they ran up against a hard enough stretch and decided the belief wasn't worth the effort. I understand that. There were periods where the argument against carrying on would have been fairly easy to make.

I kept going anyway. Through wedding photography to brand design, through brand design to web, through the years when I was doing both and neither quite well enough. Not because I had a plan, but because stopping would have felt wrong in a way I didn't have language for at the time.

Eventually the clients got bigger. Royal London. BBC. When those names first appeared in my inbox, my honest instinct was to check whether the email was real. That's the version of imposter syndrome that coexists with genuine competence — it never quite goes away, it just becomes less loud.

The belief turned out to be correct. I'm still slightly surprised by that. Not in a way that keeps me up at night. But enough that I don't take it for granted.

## Before that, though

It's 2001. The computer is an enormous beige box and the dial-up connection makes that sound — the one that's genuinely impossible to describe to anyone who didn't grow up with it. Then you're online. Not really online by any standard that would mean anything now, but connected enough to find Neopets, and to spend an unreasonable number of hours designing landing pages for other users in Macromedia Fireworks.

I'm thirteen. I have no idea this is web design. I have no idea this is *anything*. It's just the thing I do after school, while the floppy disk is in the drive and the screen is covered in guides and layers. Macromedia Fireworks doesn't care that I have no formal training — which, when you're thirteen and figuring things out, is exactly the right quality for a piece of software to have.

I lost that somewhere during college. Not deliberately — it just drifted, the way things do when you're trying to work out who you are in a context that doesn't particularly reward it. I found it again at twenty-two, sitting in a bay window in north London, with a camera bag and a blank canvas in Photoshop.

Turns out it had been there the whole time.

## Why I'm writing now

I've been doing this long enough to see patterns repeat.

Not the same clients — every client is genuinely different. But the same *structural tensions*. Brand decisions that ignore engineering constraints. Engineering choices that quietly undermine what the brand is trying to say. A visual system that's beautiful until the developer asks how it handles edge cases, and nobody in the room has a good answer.

I've sat in enough of those rooms — or enough video calls, as rooms became less common — to know that the gap between brand thinking and build thinking is where most of the expensive mistakes happen. And I've spent twelve years working in both.

That's what I want to write about here. Not tutorials. Not listicles. Notes. Observations from the work. The patterns worth putting into words because they keep showing up, and because someone else is probably sitting with the same problem right now.

Some of it will be practical — the next piece is specifically about auditing a WordPress site you didn't build, without breaking anything. Some of it will be more strategic: how brand architecture decisions compound into engineering problems, and vice versa. Things I've been meaning to write down for years.

I'm starting now because there's enough of it to say, and because writing it down is the only way I know of to actually think it through properly.
